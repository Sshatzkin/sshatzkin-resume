  Early Bird

In the winter of my second year at UChicago, I enrolled in Entrepeneurship in Technology, a Master's level course that accepts a handful of undergraduates each year.

In this class, we worked in teams to develop a startup from problem to product in 10 weeks.

Problem:

Back in the pre-COVID world, people used to work in public spaces like libraries and coffee shops. However, this comes with the risk of arriving at a busy venue without available seating or sitting down only to find out that there are no outlets to charge your laptop or terrible internet speeds. After going through the hastle of finding a workspace that satisfies their needs, most people fall into a routine of frequenting the same places, often at the same times because it is difficult to break routine and find new workspaces. Current solutions such as Yelp and Google provide statistics for businesses, but those applications prioritize proximity in their search algorithm, not workspace-relevant features like noise level, public internet access, or how comfortable the seating is.

Product:

Early Bird is a community-driven web-application that helps students find ideal study spaces and remote workers identify new public spaces to work from. Aggregating user-reported data about seating availability, wifi status, and other relevant needs and preferences, Early Bird can provide users with personalized recommendations for the best workspaces that suit their needs.   Security Incident Map

At UChicago, we regularly recieve security alerts about local safety incidents but the emails that the school provides are sparse with details and give only a street address, making it a little bit difficult to understand where and when these incidents are happening. Students deciding how to walk home from class at night or students looking for appartments off-campus would benefit from having access to this information in a user-friendly and intuitive format.

At Uncommon Hacks 2020, I decided to build a prototype of a security application using React that plots real data from the public UCPD incident archive onto a Google Maps API-driven map. Users can filter Map data by incident type, time, location to find information relevant to their needs.
