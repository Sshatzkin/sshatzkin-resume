Be Nice to Garbage Bot

Research Overview

"Be Nice to Garbage Bot" is a human-robot interaction (HRI) research study conducted by myself, Lane Painter, and Yutao Cao that explored how simple design modifications affect human willingness to assist robots in public settings. The experiment investigated whether adding googly eyes to a non-humanoid robot would increase the likelihood of people helping it with a simple task.

Experimental Design

Figure: The robot used in the study, shown with and without googly eyes.

The study was conducted at the University of Chicago's John Crerar Library using a Hello Robot Stretch - a utilitarian, non-anthropomorphic robot designed for assistive tasks. We implemented a 2x2 experimental design testing two key factors: the presence or absence of googly eyes on the robot, and whether the robot approached individuals or small groups for assistance.

The robot was programmed to attempt picking up bottles near a trash can, deliberately failing in a realistic manner before requesting help from passersby with the phrase "Could you please assist me in picking up this piece of garbage?" The experiment used a Wizard-of-Oz approach, with team members remotely controlling the robot's movements and speech through an Xbox controller and digital soundboard.

Key Findings

Our research revealed significant differences in human behavior based on the robot's appearance. Participants interacting with the eye-equipped robot showed substantially higher intervention rates compared to the no-eyes condition. While most people paused to assess the situation regardless of the robot's appearance, those encountering the robot without eyes frequently expressed confusion about the source of the voice or the robot's intentions.

Interestingly, we observed that all small groups approached by the robot chose to intervene and assist, likely due to group members discussing the situation and providing mutual reassurance about how to help. This finding suggests that group dynamics can overcome individual hesitation in human-robot interactions.

Research Impact

This study contributes valuable insights to the growing field of human-robot interaction, particularly as service robots become more prevalent in public spaces like hospitals, restaurants, and hotels. The research demonstrates that simple, low-cost design modifications - such as adding eyes - can significantly improve a robot's ability to receive assistance from humans when needed.

The findings have practical implications for robotics designers, suggesting that even non-anthropomorphic robots benefit from minimal anthropomorphic features that help humans understand how to interact with them. As autonomous robots increasingly operate in public environments, understanding these interaction dynamics becomes crucial for their successful deployment and acceptance.
